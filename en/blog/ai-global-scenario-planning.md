# AI Global Scenario Planning: A Chinese Perspective

This essay responds to OpenAI Global Affairs' December 2025 article, [AI Global Scenario-Planning](https://openaiglobalaffairs.substack.com/p/ai-global-scenario-planning). The original uses compute capacity and AI adoption as two axes for thinking about competition between the United States and China.

It is a useful framework, although its definition of success reflects the priorities of an American AI company. Reading it from China changes some of the questions.

## The four scenarios

OpenAI describes four possible outcomes for the following two years:

1. **America's AI Century**: The United States removes power and permitting bottlenecks, expands compute, and strengthens its lead in models and adoption.
2. **Global AI with Chinese Characteristics**: Chinese breakthroughs, weaker export controls, and a US slowdown allow China to lead across chips, models, and deployment.
3. **The US Standard**: US companies retain a model and trust advantage even if domestic compute growth slows.
4. **China's AI+ World**: The United States leads in compute while China gains share through cost, customization, and large-scale deployment.

The scenarios are not forecasts. They are a way to identify which assumptions matter: access to chips, power and data centers, model capability, cost, domestic adoption, international trust, and distribution.

## What the framework gets right

Compute and deployment cannot be separated. A country may train strong models but struggle to make them affordable or useful. Another may lag at the frontier while building products that reach more factories, devices, and public services.

The framework also treats adoption as more than consumer usage. Enterprise procurement, government policy, technical standards, financing, and diplomatic relationships all affect which systems become embedded in a market.

This is especially relevant to China. Its domestic market and manufacturing base give local companies many places to deploy AI. Semiconductor restrictions, however, continue to constrain the amount and type of compute available. Both facts can be true at the same time.

## A Chinese reading of the scenarios

### Semiconductor pressure can accelerate investment, but it still imposes a cost

Export restrictions have pushed Chinese companies and the government to invest in domestic chips, software stacks, and supply chains. Huawei and other firms show that pressure can produce substitutes.

That does not make the constraint disappear. Advanced fabrication equipment, high-bandwidth memory, yields, interconnects, developer tooling, and energy efficiency are separate problems. Progress in one layer does not automatically solve the rest.

Scenario planning should therefore track measurable capacity rather than assume that pressure guarantees either failure or self-sufficiency.

### Deployment may matter as much as frontier rankings

China has a plausible advantage in applying models to manufacturing, logistics, consumer hardware, vehicles, and public infrastructure. Cost and customization can matter more than benchmark leadership in these settings.

This makes the fourth scenario worth taking seriously. A Chinese company can gain influence by shipping useful systems at scale even when a US model remains more capable on a benchmark.

Deployment is not a consolation prize. It creates revenue, operating data, distribution channels, and engineering experience. Still, weak access to frontier compute can eventually limit what those products can do, so the two races remain connected.

### Trust differs by market

OpenAI describes a trust deficit for Chinese technology in important markets. That is clear in the United States and parts of Europe, where security reviews and geopolitical policy can block adoption.

Other countries weigh the issue differently. Price, financing, local support, data rules, and existing trade relationships may carry more weight. Chinese providers can compete strongly where they offer affordable deployment and customization.

Trust does not vanish in those markets. Governments still care about dependence, privacy, sovereignty, and the ability to audit systems. The relevant question is which provider offers an acceptable combination of capability, cost, control, and political risk.

### Coordination is an advantage with its own failure modes

China can coordinate infrastructure investment across government, state-owned enterprises, universities, and private companies. That can speed up data-center construction, power allocation, procurement, and deployment.

Central coordination can also misallocate capital or protect weak projects from market feedback. Competition in the United States produces duplication, but it also lets different technical approaches fail and succeed independently.

The comparison is therefore less tidy than coordination versus chaos. Each system has a different way of financing experiments, absorbing failure, and changing direction.

## What I would monitor

I would avoid assigning precise probabilities to the four scenarios. The inputs are too uncertain, and neat numbers can create confidence without evidence. A smaller set of indicators is more useful:

1. China's supply of advanced accelerators, memory, interconnects, and data-center power.
2. Training and inference cost for comparable Chinese and US models.
3. Adoption in manufacturing, government, consumer devices, and enterprise software.
4. International market share, separated by region and sector.
5. Restrictions on chips, cloud access, investment, and model exports.
6. Whether domestic AI use produces measurable productivity gains.

These indicators can also reveal mixed outcomes. The United States may lead in frontier models, China in industrial deployment, and regional providers in language or regulatory niches. A single winner is only one possible shape of the market.

## My conclusion

OpenAI's framework is useful because it turns a vague geopolitical contest into a set of assumptions that can be checked. Its American perspective is visible in the way it defines leadership, especially the emphasis on sustaining a US lead.

From a Chinese perspective, the strongest case rests on deployment: building affordable systems, integrating them into the physical economy, and adapting them to markets that US providers may serve poorly. That path still depends on compute and cannot be separated from semiconductor policy.

The likely result may be uneven. One country can lead in training, another in hardware deployment, and both can dominate different regions. The framework becomes more useful once leadership is treated as several measurable advantages rather than one global title.
